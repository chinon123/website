The Mairie de Paris is working to reduce the sensation of heat (cf. urban heat island phenomena) and has initiated a feasibility study for access to water with a group from the Paris 1 sustainable development master's programme in order to quantify the exact needs. This study contributed to the opening in 2017 of the innovative open-water swimming pool at the Villette basin.

Abstract

In Paris, we can assess that 3% of the area is physical water; however, regarding the year 2003 heatwave and 15 000 dead people, it seems not enough. How Paris can tackle this kind of urban heat island’ phenomena touching every major city and the most fragile populations?

With a team of economist, geographer and jurist, we propose the idea that urban planning and urban furniture (like a fountain, for example) can decrease this effect.

Thanks to different kind of tools (enquiries, field study, and survey), we develop one binary concept: “dry area” and “wet area” to highlight our idea and defend our position: urban amenity can increase water physical access and thus decrease urban heat island phenomena.

A water fountain is a very interesting object. It offers water access, and it is often viewed as a urban amenety. One of the conclusions behind the study is to reshape this object to increase its value in the urban landscape.
